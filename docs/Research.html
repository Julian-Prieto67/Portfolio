INTRODUCTION

As part of the Collective Embodied Intelligence Lab I worked on a control algorithm for robotic swarms. I facilitated all hardware equipment, performed repairs on the chassis of robots, and modified the experiment setup. The robots were programmed in Microchip studio using C. The experiments I carried out proved that robots moving randomly in a swarm could be controlled by changing their shape and sensing their neighbors. The name of the paper is "The Robo-Physics of Distributed Shape Change Algorithms in Stochastic Collectives" and it has since been submitted to the 2025 IEEE International Conference on Robotics and Automation (ICRA).

ABSTRACT

Years of observing biological life have taught researchers the importance of shape on collectives like proteins and enzymes. This project aims to translate these strategies toward robotics. By manipulating shape through algorithms that react to an individualâ€™s surroundings, a collective of robots can exhibit predictable and controllable behaviors that contribute to a larger goal. By using swarms that are cheap, have simple rules, and a statistically significant outcome, we can create a robust and effective system that can be implemented in exploration missions or medicinal nano-technology.

THE THEORY

The simulation shown is a simplistic model of a swarm of shape changing robots moving randomly. Given enough collisions, they shape change to a rectangle and after enough time has passed they move from the more crowded area to the more spacious room no the right without any directional input. This is statistically significant and controllable behavior. Once this was proved in simulation, the goal was to perform this narrow hallway test in real life. I set up a rectangular arena that could be customized with plastic dividers to emulate the narrow hallway simulation above. The robots were to be placed in this arena and their interactions and behavior were recorded and analyzed using mounted april-tags.

MY PART

On the left are the robot agents used for the hardware experiment. They utilize a toy ball designed for pets that simulate random brownian motion by driving an internal motor with an off-center counterweight. On top of this wild body is a lightweight hub 3D printed from PETG. The shape-changes are carried out by microservos actuating arms that pull on a paper skirt to change into three shapes: an oblong shape, a star shape, and a convex shape. Each Hub weighed 15g including the PCB, microservos, and sensors. The chassis was incredibly light and fragile which made assembly a delicate process. The skirts were cut from a cardstock paper and glued to the hub. Despite the ligthweight design, the robots had a difficult time moving the weight of the hub and as a result I had to design a solution to make the robots move more efficiently.

My Experiment

In order to influence more motion in the agents, we decided to modify the counterweight in each of the wild bodies. I performed several trials with varying weights and was able to show that the robots moved more efficiently with a heavier counterweight. This motion was quantified by the mean squared displacement graph shown below. The raw trajectories of the robots these curves are derived from are shown below.

The weight was added in a quick experiment by using increasing amounts of small neodymium magnets. The magnets were placed on the counterweight, which is a small piece of mild steel, and the wild body was tested in the arena. The results showed that a bigger counterweight could move more mass with a drop off in motion once the internal motor could no longer fit the magnets. This was a simple solution to a complex problem and it was a great example of testing out any idea as a potential solution. Once the mass of the counterweight was decided, a more denser alloy of steel was used to make a new counterweight that replaced the old ones.
